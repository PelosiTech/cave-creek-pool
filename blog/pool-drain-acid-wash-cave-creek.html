Cave Creek Pool Drain & Acid Wash: When and Why

By Cave Creek Pool Pros | Published February 28, 2026

If your Cave Creek pool's plaster has turned gray, developed stubborn stains, or feels rougher than sandpaper under your feet, you may be hearing terms like "drain and acid wash" from pool professionals. It's one of the most effective restoration procedures for plaster pools, but it's also one of the most commonly misunderstood. Here's what Cave Creek pool owners need to know about when an acid wash is truly necessary, what the process involves, and how to ensure it's done safely and effectively.

What Is a Pool Acid Wash?

An acid wash — technically called an acid rinse or drain-and-clean — involves completely draining the pool and applying a diluted muriatic acid solution (typically a 50/50 mix of acid and water) to the exposed plaster surface. The acid dissolves a thin layer of the plaster's surface, removing embedded stains, mineral deposits, algae staining, and discoloration to reveal fresh, white plaster underneath.

Think of it as aggressive exfoliation for your pool. Each acid wash removes approximately 1/16 to 1/8 inch of plaster material. A standard plaster pool starts with about 3/8 to 1/2 inch of plaster, meaning a pool can typically handle 3 to 5 acid washes over its lifetime before the plaster becomes too thin and needs replastering.

When Cave Creek Pools Need an Acid Wash

Several conditions common to Cave Creek pools indicate it's time for an acid wash. First, persistent staining that regular chemical treatment can't remove. Cave Creek's mineral-rich water causes iron, copper, and manganese staining that often penetrates the plaster surface. If vitamin C treatment (ascorbic acid applied directly to stains) doesn't resolve metal staining, an acid wash is the next step.

Second, widespread calcium scale that has bonded to the plaster surface. While localized scale can be addressed with pumice stones or spot acid treatment, widespread scaling — common after years of Cave Creek's hard water — requires a full acid wash for uniform results.

Third, algae that has stained the plaster. If your Cave Creek pool experienced a severe algae bloom (particularly black algae, which penetrates plaster with root-like structures), the discoloration often remains after the algae itself is killed. An acid wash removes the stained plaster layer, eliminating both the discoloration and any dormant algae beneath the surface.

Fourth, general gray or dingy appearance. Over time, even well-maintained Cave Creek pools develop a gradual darkening as minerals, body oils, sunscreen residue, and environmental contaminants accumulate in the plaster's porous surface. If your pool looks dirty even when the water is chemically balanced and clear, an acid wash can restore its original brightness.

The Drain and Acid Wash Process

The procedure requires careful planning and execution. Here's what a professional acid wash involves in Cave Creek. First, the pool is drained completely using a submersible pump, with water discharged according to Maricopa County regulations — typically to the sewer cleanout, never to the street or desert wash where it could harm native vegetation and wildlife.

Once drained, the crew inspects the empty pool for structural issues — cracks, delamination, hollow spots in the plaster, and the condition of fittings and drain covers. This inspection while the pool is empty is valuable because these issues are invisible underwater.

The acid wash itself involves wetting a section of plaster with water, applying the acid solution from top to bottom, allowing it to react for 30 to 60 seconds (the surface will fizz as acid dissolves calcium in the plaster), then rinsing thoroughly with water. The process moves systematically around the pool, section by section, with the acid residue pumped out of the deep end as it accumulates.

The entire drain-and-acid-wash process typically takes one full day for an average-sized Cave Creek residential pool (10,000 to 20,000 gallons). Refilling begins immediately after the wash is complete — it's critical that the pool doesn't sit empty in Cave Creek's heat, as thermal expansion and hydrostatic pressure from groundwater can cause a drained pool shell to crack or even lift out of the ground.

Important Considerations for Cave Creek

Timing matters significantly in Cave Creek. The best months for a drain and acid wash are October through March, when temperatures are moderate. Draining a pool in July when the concrete deck is 160°F creates real risks of plaster damage from rapid temperature changes and makes the work dangerous for technicians handling acid in extreme heat.

Hydrostatic pressure is a concern in parts of Cave Creek with high water tables, particularly properties near Cave Creek Wash or in lower-lying areas. A hydrostatic relief valve in the pool's main drain helps prevent the pool shell from "floating" when empty, but this valve must be verified as functional before draining begins.

After refilling, the startup chemistry process is critical. Fresh plaster surfaces (and freshly acid-washed surfaces) leach calcium and alkalinity into the water for 30 to 60 days. During this period, pH will rise rapidly and the water will tend toward cloudiness. Careful chemical management during startup prevents staining and etching of the freshly revealed plaster surface.

DIY vs. Professional Acid Wash

While some pool maintenance can be handled by homeowners, acid washing is one procedure best left to professionals. The risks include hydrochloric acid burns, toxic fume inhalation, plaster damage from improper technique, structural damage from draining improperly, and environmental violations from improper wastewater disposal. An experienced pool service company carries the proper insurance, equipment, training, and Maricopa County disposal permits to handle the job safely and correctly.
